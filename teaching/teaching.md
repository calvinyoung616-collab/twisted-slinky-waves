# Teaching Notes for the Twisted Slinky Experiment

## 1. Overview

This document provides practical guidance for using the twisted slinky experiment in teaching. In this setup, the bottom of a slinky is fixed, the top is twisted through several turns, and then released. Two kinds of motion can be observed:

- **torsional wave**: rotation of the coils about the slinky axis propagating along the slinky;
- **transverse wave**: the propagation of lateral motion associated with the motion of the coil centers in the horizontal plane.

This experiment is well suited for undergraduate teaching in mechanics, oscillations, and waves. It is visually striking, easy to set up, and rich in physical content, including wave propagation, reflection, non-sinusoidal motion, and coupled response.

---

## 2. Main phenomena to observe

### 2.1 Torsional wave
From the top view, the **top point** begins to rotate back immediately after release. The disturbance propagates downward along the slinky and is reflected at the fixed end.

The motion of the top point is clearly non-sinusoidal. In the idealized picture, the **angular velocity of the top point as a function of time is approximately square-wave-like**, while the corresponding angular displacement is approximately sawtooth-like.

### 2.2 Transverse wave
From the side view, one can observe lateral motion of the coil centers. This motion also propagates along the slinky and appears as a **transverse wave**.

When the initial twist angle is small, the transverse wave is often difficult to observe clearly. As the initial twist angle increases, the transverse motion becomes more visible.

---

## 3. Recommended apparatus

The following equipment is recommended:

- a **plastic slinky**;
- transparent tape or another reliable fixing method for the bottom end;
- a high-speed camera, or a phone capable of high-frame-rate recording;
- auxiliary lighting;
- a small **reflective sticker** for torsional-wave measurements;
- a **black curtain** or other dark matte background for transverse-wave measurements;
- video analysis software such as **Tracker**.

---

## 4. Important experimental notes

### 4.1 Use a plastic slinky rather than a steel one
A plastic slinky is strongly recommended for this experiment.

Compared with a plastic slinky, a steel slinky typically has **higher density** and **smaller shear modulus** in the present context. When the cross-sectional shear force cannot provide sufficient centripetal force for the rotational motion, the lateral displacement of the coils tends to grow larger and larger until the slinky collapses. As a result, a steel slinky is much more difficult to use for a clean reproduction of the phenomena discussed in this experiment.

For teaching purposes, a plastic slinky is usually more robust, more stable, and much easier to control.

### 4.2 The initial twist angle should be neither too small nor too large
The initial twist angle must be chosen within a reasonable range.

- If it is **too small**, the main phenomena, especially the transverse wave, may be too weak to observe clearly.
- If it is **too large**, the slinky may undergo irreversible deformation, or the motion amplitude may become so large that the slinky collapses. In the worst case, the slinky may be permanently damaged and lost for future use.

A moderate initial twist angle is therefore recommended for classroom demonstrations.

### 4.3 Avoid giving an unintended lateral push
When releasing the top, do not introduce an additional sideways push. Otherwise, the observed transverse motion may be contaminated by the release method rather than generated by the slinky dynamics themselves.

### 4.4 Keep the bottom boundary condition as stable as possible
The bottom end should be fixed firmly and reproducibly. A loose or shifting bottom constraint can affect both the reflection of the torsional wave and the development of the transverse wave.

### 4.5 Frame rate matters
If the camera frame rate is too low, the accuracy of period measurements is strongly affected, especially for the transverse wave.

A frame rate of **at least 200 fps** is recommended. Higher frame rates are preferable when available.

---

## 5. Notes for using Tracker

If automatic tracking in Tracker is to be used, the contrast between the slinky and the background should be increased as much as possible.

### 5.1 Torsional-wave measurement
For measurements of the torsional wave, it is recommended to attach a **small reflective sticker** near the top point. This makes the top point much easier to detect and track automatically in the top-view video.

### 5.2 Transverse-wave measurement
For measurements of the transverse wave, it is recommended to place a **black curtain** or other dark matte background behind the slinky. This improves the visibility of the side contour and makes it easier to track the lateral motion.

Good contrast is essential for reliable automatic tracking.

---

## 6. Suggested experimental procedure

### 6.1 Top-view recording for the torsional wave
1. Fix the bottom of the slinky to a horizontal surface.
2. Attach a small reflective sticker near the top point.
3. Place the camera above the slinky.
4. Twist the top through a chosen angle.
5. Release the top carefully without adding a lateral push.
6. Record the motion.

This view is most useful for observing:

- propagation and reflection of the torsional wave;
- non-sinusoidal motion of the top point;
- the fact that the angular velocity of the top point is approximately square-wave-like in time.

### 6.2 Side-view recording for the transverse wave
1. Keep the bottom fixed.
2. Place the camera at the side of the slinky.
3. Use a black curtain or dark matte background.
4. Illuminate the slinky clearly.
5. Twist and release as before.
6. Record the motion.

This view is most useful for observing:

- propagation of the transverse wave;
- dependence of the transverse-wave period on the initial twist angle;
- possible collapse when the twist is too large or the slinky material is unsuitable.

---

## 7. Suggested classroom or lab activities

### Activity A: Qualitative observation
Ask students to observe both top-view and side-view videos and answer:

- Does the slinky relax everywhere at once after release?
- What is the most obvious motion immediately after release?
- How does the motion change as the initial twist angle increases?

### Activity B: Measure the period of the torsional wave
From the top-view video:

- track the top point;
- identify successive reversals;
- estimate the period of the torsional wave;
- compare results for different initial twist angles.

Students should find that the period of the torsional wave is approximately independent of the initial twist angle.

### Activity C: Measure the period of the transverse wave
From the side-view video:

- track the lateral motion at a selected height;
- determine the first transverse-wave period after release;
- repeat for different initial twist angles.

Students should find that the transverse-wave period depends on the initial twist angle.

## 8. Questions for further exploration

1. **Height variation during twisting**  
   Because of the geometry of the slinky, its height changes continuously during twisting and untwisting. Try to derive the relation between the height and time, and design an experiment to test it.

2. **Damped torsional wave**  
   Suppose the torsional-wave equation contains a damping term of the form  
   \[
   -\beta \dot{u}.
   \]
   What is the solution of the damped torsional-wave equation? If the transverse wave is driven by this damped torsional wave, can the increase of the transverse-wave period with time be explained quantitatively?

3. **Different materials**  
   Repeat the experiment using slinkies made of different materials. What changes in the torsional wave and the transverse wave? How do density, elastic modulus, and shear modulus affect the observed behavior?

4. **Trajectory of the top coil center**  
   Using the centroid-tracking function in Tracker, design an experiment to measure the trajectory of the center of mass of the topmost coil. How is this trajectory related to the torsional wave and the transverse wave?

---

## 9. Notes for instructors

This experiment works well at several levels.

- At an introductory level, it can be used to demonstrate wave propagation, reflection, and non-sinusoidal motion.
- At an intermediate level, students can measure periods and compare them with theory.
- At an advanced level, students can analyze driven transverse response, damping, and the limitations of simplified models.

It is usually effective to let students first describe what they observe before introducing theory.

---

## 10. Safety and handling

- Do not overstretch the slinky.
- Do not use excessively large twist angles.
- Keep the surrounding area clear during release.
- Handle the slinky carefully if only one specimen is available, since excessive deformation may permanently damage it.
